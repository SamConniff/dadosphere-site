What the FAQ

the short version

  1. This is not an advice or support service. It's an experiment in authentic intelligence: a searchable database of honest insight, lived experience and real support services.
  2. Every answer is submitted by a real man and human-moderated before it's matched to one of 10 themes research links to men's mental health.
  3. Ask any question and those same 10 themes find you an answer. It won't be a reply to your exact question. It's honest insight from another man that might just help.
  4. You can submit questions, answers, or suggest services. A human moderates everything before it goes anywhere near the site.
  5. The Dadosphere is 100% anonymous. Answers are stored on a UK server with no personal identifying information, ever.

the longer version

The Dadosphere, see what you did there. Is this an online space for aggressive dads pushing dad supremacy and undermining mums?

Nope. That sounds terrible. It's a website full of honest answers from men, written in response to the important questions other men are actually asking.

Like a chat room, old school?

Also no. No usernames, no keyboard warriors, no opportunity for abuse. Every answer is moderated before it's published.

So I can't use the karatehamster69 handle I've had since AOL dial-up?

Big no. But you can ask anything, completely anonymously. Research suggests 80% of men have a difficult question and nowhere to ask it.

They probably don't want to look stupid, asking bollocks, looking like a pussy.

In a manner of speaking, but let's be kinder than that. Being honest about hard feelings, and getting trusted answers, is linked to better mental health in men. So ask whatever you like, anonymously, and we'll match the themes in your question to real answers from other men's lived experience. Not a reply to your exact words, more a prompt that might help.

Bit like a confession box, but with a random bloke instead of a priest?

Zero guilt, zero judgement, but yes-ish.

Why not just ask ChatGPT?

You could. But this is an experiment in authentic intelligence. Real men have answered real questions about the vicissitudes of being a man, mapped against ten themes linked to how men actually fare in life. So whatever you ask is met with real wisdom, from real men.

Vicissitudes, great word. What's it mean?

Thanks. The highs and lows, the scars and medals, the adventures and anxieties of the rich school of war men have lived through. There's endless talk about the need for positive role models, and plenty about the online forces selling men cheap answers to real concerns, but barely a word about the one influencer no man can unsubscribe from: his dad.

Are you talking about the manosphere?

Part of it. The manosphere is very good at meeting men's frustrations. It just sells short-term answers, usually on a monthly subscription. Some land at first, but over time they curdle, because the real problems were never going to be fixed by a six-pack or by blaming women.

So this is the answer?

It's thousands of answers, vetted, moderated, and completely unconnected to anyone's sales pitch. Submitted by men, for other men.

Honestly, I've got no idea what my dad really thinks.

Same. And here's the irony. The longest-running study on earth has shown, without doubt, that close relationships shape everything from happiness to how long we live, and yet male friendship is in freefall. 15% of men now report having no close friends, a number that's quadrupled since the 90s. When men do talk, it's at the match, on a walk, at the pub, and rarely deeply.

Then if it's all about relationships, why another bloody website? Why not just go to the pub?

Love to, mine's a Guinness. But actually I've got bathtime, then a bit of work, and honestly I'm shattered, can we try in a few weeks? That. That's the whole problem. Life happens, men deprioritise the very relationships that would help, and the spaces to talk quietly disappear. The Dadosphere tries to offer the next best thing, the kind of adjacent honesty men often find easier, in the sort of online space the evidence says can genuinely help.

But aren't there already loads of these? Can't move for men's groups and blokes "opening up" on podcasts.

True. And yet suicide is still the biggest killer of men under 50, violence against women hasn't shifted, and most men, good men, say they feel blamed for it all. For all the noise, there's still no coordinated, long-term plan for the men quietly in crisis. This is one small attempt at one.

Alright. How do I actually submit something?

Tap give strength → and you can do three things: ask a question, answer one, or suggest a support service you rate. A human reads everything before it goes live. Nothing auto-publishes. Your answer gets matched to other men by theme, and it's never traced back to you.

And if I read something that worries me, or I'm just not okay right now?

Then please don't rely on a website. The Dadosphere isn't a crisis service and never pretends to be. Every page points you to people who are, any time of day or night: tap support → at the top. If you're in danger right now, call 999. If you need to talk, Samaritans are on 116 123, free, 24/7, and you can text SHOUT to 85258. Reaching out isn't weak. It's the single bravest, most useful thing in here.

Hang on, who's behind all this?

A dad called Sam Conniff. South London, a few kids, wrote a couple of books you've probably not read (Be More Pirate is one). He kept noticing the questions men carry round for years and never get asked out loud, so he started asking them. Hundreds of men answered, honestly, and this is what they said. No brand, no guru, no monthly subscription. Just one bloke who reckoned other blokes deserved a better question.

Fine. I'll have a look.

Good. The Guinness is still on me.

Watch out, lads, here come the Dads.

Ha, sort of. Watch out, manosphere, here comes the Dadosphere.

I preferred mine.

Me too.
